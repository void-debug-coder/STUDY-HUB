🏗️ Module 1: Complete Notes

1. Introduction to Strength of Materials

Rigid vs Deformable Bodies

  • Rigid Body: No deformation under load. Used in Statics.
  • Deformable Body: Changes shape/size under load. Real materials deform first, then fail.

Key Properties

  • Strength: Ability to resist failure under external force
  • Stiffness: Ability to resist deformation
  • Stability: Ability to maintain equilibrium

Material Assumptions

  • Homogeneous: Same properties throughout
  • Isotropic: Same properties in all directions
  • Continuous: No voids or cracks

2. Simple Stress & Strain

Normal Stress σ = P/A

  • Force per unit area ⊥ to cross-section
  • Tensile: +ve, pulls material apart
  • Compressive: -ve, pushes material together
  • Units: N/mm² = MPa, N/m² = Pa

Shear Stress τ = V/A

  • Force per unit area || to cross-section
  • Causes sliding of layers
  • Single shear: τ = P/A, Double shear: τ = P/2A

Normal Strain ε = ΔL/L

  • Ratio of change in length to original length
  • Dimensionless, often given as % or μ-strain
  • Tensile strain: +ve elongation
  • Compressive strain: -ve shortening

3. Hooke's Law & Elastic Constants

Hooke's Law: σ = Eε

  • Valid within elastic limit: stress ∝ strain
  • E = Young's Modulus: Modulus of elasticity
  • Steel: E ≈ 200 GPa, Concrete: E ≈ 25 GPa, Aluminum: E ≈ 70 GPa

Stress-Strain Curve for Mild Steel

  • Proportional Limit: End of linear region
  • Elastic Limit: No permanent deformation if unloaded
  • Yield Point: ~250 MPa, strain ~0.125%. Large deformation begins
  • Ultimate Strength: ~400 MPa, maximum stress
  • Rupture: Failure, strain ~20-25%

Poisson's Ratio μ = -Lateral Strain / Axial Strain

  • Ratio of lateral contraction to axial elongation
  • Steel: μ ≈ 0.3, Concrete: μ ≈ 0.2, Rubber: μ ≈ 0.5
  • 0 ≤ μ ≤ 0.5 for engineering materials

Shear Modulus G = τ/γ

  • Modulus of rigidity, relates shear stress to shear strain
  • G = E / 2(1+μ)

Bulk Modulus K = Volumetric Stress / Volumetric Strain

  • Resistance to uniform compression
  • K = E / 3(1-2μ)

Relation Between E, G, K, μ

E = 2G(1+μ) = 3K(1-2μ)
E = 9KG / (3K+G)

4. Axial Deformation

Deformation of Prismatic Bar: δ = PL/AE

  • P = Axial load, L = Length, A = Area, E = Young's modulus
  • For stepped bars: δ_total = Σ(PL/AE) for each segment

Thermal Stress: σ = EαΔT

  • α = Coefficient of thermal expansion per °C
  • If free to expand: δ_T = αLΔT, no stress
  • If restrained: Stress develops. Expansion restrained → compression

5. Equilibrium & Support Reactions

Conditions of Equilibrium

  • ΣF_x = 0, ΣF_y = 0, ΣM = 0
  • Free Body Diagram: Isolate body, show ALL external forces + reactions

Types of Supports

  • Roller: 1 reaction ⊥ to surface
  • Pin/Hinge: 2 reactions: R_x, R_y
  • Fixed: 3 reactions: R_x, R_y, M

6. Biaxial Stress & Principal Stresses

Stress on Inclined Plane

σ_n = (σ_x + σ_y)/2 + (σ_x - σ_y)/2 × cos2θ + τ_xy × sin2θ
τ_n = -(σ_x - σ_y)/2 × sin2θ + τ_xy × cos2θ

Principal Stresses

  • Planes where shear stress = 0
  • σ_1,2 = (σ_x + σ_y)/2 ± √[((σ_x - σ_y)/2)² + τ_xy²]
  • Max shear: τ_max = (σ_1 - σ_2)/2

7. Key Assumptions for Module 1

  • Loads applied through centroid to avoid bending
  • Cross-section remains plane after deformation
  • Material obeys Hooke's Law
  • Deformations are small
